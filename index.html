About me

As you’ve probably guessed already, my name is Georgia! I’m twenty-five and I’m from London, but also spent part of my childhood living in Sao Paulo, Brazil. When I’m not writing code, I enjoy going to the theatre, watching Game of Thrones and practising yoga. Since graduating in 2016, I’ve volunteered at the Rio Olympics, worked on a cruise ship, and until recently I was working at a FinTech startup. Over the last few months, I have been teaching myself to code full time.

I'm also a Scout Leader and have been involved in Scouting for the past fifteen years. We’re currently working on our World Challenge Badge, which requires the Scouts to spend time volunteering within the local community, to explore environmental and international issues and to make contact with Scouts in a different country. I love bringing subjects to life for our young adults. Recently, I ran a meeting where we analysed UN data on the experiences of women in different parts of the world. We began the evening by playing a few mixed gender team games, which were then rewarded with sweets. The girls quickly realised they had been ‘under-rewarded’ compared to the boys, and this sparked outrage! We discussed how this made them feel, and asked everyone if girls and boys should receive equal reward for equal effort - and got a resounding yes! (And don’t worry, the girls were fairly compensated at the end of the meeting).

My interest in coding

I’ve always been an inquisitive person, and the CodeFirst:Girls course I took last year fuelled my curiosity in programming. I’d considered many careers while at university and after graduating, but hadn’t found something that felt right for me. Coding is the creative outlet I had been searching for; I left my first CodeFirst:Girls meeting feeling like a door had been opened into a whole new world, and that passion for coding has stayed with me. Since then, I’ve completed a Python course with CodeFirst:Girls, and I'm currently working on a twelve week programme to get my Scouts interested in coding while they earn a Digital Maker Badge. I’m also creating a website where Scouts can play games that teach them to code - it’s still in the design stages, but I’m hoping to get it running over the summer.

Working at a startup, I enjoyed being encouraged to solve problems, to take responsibility and to challenge myself and others. I’ve seen how collaborative and hard working tech teams can be, and know I would thrive in an environment where I can work closely with others while coding and being creative.

Why Founders and Coders

Working at a startup, I have also witnessed the lack of diversity in the tech industry. Learning to code myself, and teaching my Scouts (half of whom are girls) to code, is my small way of making a change. I know that Founders and Coders is an open and inclusive community that embraces diversity, which is the kind of environment I want to learn in.

I like how Founders and Coders gives its developers exposure to clients from the nonprofit sector and with this, the chance to work on projects that could potentially make a real difference to people’s lives. Also, having the opportunity to return as a mentor (both in London and possibly abroad), and to contribute to the curriculum and the learning of others seems like a great opportunity and something I’d really enjoy doing.

About my website

What's gone well

I’m really happy with the aesthetics of my website, and I feel much more confident in my knowledge of HTML and CSS now. I feel like I’ve come a long way from the website I made on my CodeFirst:Girls course last year, which was really not very mobile friendly at all! So I’m proud that this one works well on both mobile and desktop, and everywhere in between. I’ve enjoyed flexing my Javascript skills on this project and have worked hard to use a few different examples - it’s been tough, but when you see your code working and your website come to life, it’s so rewarding!

What I can improve on

If I was starting this project again, I would definitely go for a mobile first approach. I didn’t realise how challenging it would be to go back and add mobile designs afterwards and in hindsight, I should have started with mobile and scaled up from there. I’m happy with the colours I’ve chosen, and feel like they give my website a clean, fresh and minimalistic feel. However, while I have no problems viewing the website, I’m also aware this might not be the case for people who have visual impairments. In the future, I’ll give more thought to creating an accessible colour scheme, therefore making sure my work is as user friendly as possible. I feel like I could have been more structured in my naming of classes and ids and while I feel my structure is clear enough, it’s definitely something I’ll focus on more as I progress. I think all three of these improvement points have highlighted how important it is to allocate time for planning and designing at the beginning of every project - it might feel like you’re starting too slowly at first, but it saves a lot of headache later on!
